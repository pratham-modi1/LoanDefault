STAGE 5: ADVANCED OPTIMIZATION & A/B TESTING
Objective
Following the baseline modeling phase, all algorithms (Logistic Regression, Random Forest, XGBoost) hit a performance ceiling with a PR-AUC of approximately 0.364. While Recall was strong (~73%), Precision hovered at an unacceptable ~22%, meaning nearly 4 out of 5 default predictions were false alarms.

The objective of Stage 5 was to break past this mathematical ceiling and aggressively push Precision higher without collapsing Recall, utilizing Feature Engineering, Hyperparameter Tuning, and rigorous A/B testing of our preprocessing pipeline.

Phase 1: Feature Engineering (Breaking the Ceiling)
The Problem:
Tree-based algorithms are literal. When fed 30-day, 60-day, and 90-day late payment counts as separate variables, the model struggles to inherently understand the compounding severity of chronic delinquency.

The Solution:
Domain-specific financial features were engineered to provide "smarter" signals to the model, translating raw data into direct indicators of financial stress.

Total_Late_Events: A simple sum of all late payment columns to represent the raw volume of delinquency.

Weighted_Late_Score: Applied a severity multiplier (30-day*1 + 60-day*2 + 90-day*3). This mathematically forced the model to penalize severe 90-day delinquencies exponentially harder than minor 30-day delays.

Credit_Utilization_Warning: A binary flag (0 or 1) triggered if a user's credit utilization exceeded 85%, creating a sharp decision boundary for extreme debt.

Income_Per_Dependent: Calculated by dividing Monthly Income by (NumberOfDependents + 1). This normalized income to reflect actual purchasing power and household financial strain.

The Result:
Applying these features immediately broke the PR-AUC ceiling. The XGBoost baseline PR-AUC rose from 0.364 to 0.368, proving the model found genuine new predictive value in the engineered columns.

Phase 2: Hyperparameter Tuning (Optimizing for Precision)
The Problem:
Even with smarter features, the model's base configuration was still optimized for standard log-loss, not our specific business need of balancing Precision and Recall.

The Solution:
A RandomizedSearchCV was implemented across 60 fits (20 combinations × 3 cross-validation folds). Crucially, the tuner was instructed to strictly optimize for average_precision (PR-AUC) rather than accuracy or standard F1.

Key Tuned Parameters:

max_depth: 5

learning_rate: 0.01

n_estimators: 400

min_child_weight: 20 (High value explicitly chosen to prevent the model from memorizing noise).

The Result:
The tuned XGBoost model achieved a PR-AUC of 0.3762. After threshold optimization (Threshold = 0.7938), Precision surged to 39.26% and F1 reached 0.4234, while Recall settled at 45.95%.

Phase 3: Rigorous A/B Testing
Despite the improvements, an aggressive push was made to see if we could hit a 50/50 Precision/Recall split. We hypothesized that our Stage 1 MonthlyIncome imputation (using a Random Forest algorithm) might be overcomplicating the data and introducing noise, and that a simpler statistical approach might yield cleaner signals.

We conducted two isolated A/B tests against our Tuned XGBoost model.

Test A: Model Imputation vs. Median Imputation
Action: Replaced the Random Forest imputer for MonthlyIncome with a simple Median imputer.

Result: PR-AUC dropped to 0.3754 and optimal Precision dropped back to 36.73%.

Conclusion - Median Fails: The hypothesis was proven false. Income in this dataset is highly correlated with age and debt. The Random Forest imputer's nuanced, educated guesses were mathematically superior to a blanket average. The median imputer created a massive, artificial clump of identical data that actively confused the XGBoost tree splits.

Test B: Feature Pruning (Combating Multicollinearity)
Action: While keeping Median imputation, we dropped the three original raw late payment columns (30-59Days, 60-89Days, 90DaysLate) to see if they were creating an "echo chamber" alongside our new engineered Weighted_Late_Score.

Result: PR-AUC recovered slightly to 0.3758 and Precision improved to 37.21%.

Conclusion - Pruning Succeeds: Dropping the redundant columns reduced multicollinearity. By pruning the dataset, XGBoost was forced to rely strictly on the "smart" engineered features rather than getting confused between raw and engineered data.

Phase 4: The Final "God Build" Architecture
Guided by the A/B testing results, the ultimate data pipeline was finalized by combining the mathematically proven winners of each phase:

Imputation: Random Forest Imputer for complex variables (MonthlyIncome); Median for simple discrete variables (NumberOfDependents).

Feature Engineering: Added Weighted_Late_Score, Total_Late_Events, Income_Per_Dependent, and Credit_Utilization_Warning.

Feature Pruning: Dropped original raw late-payment columns to eliminate multicollinearity and noise.

Scaling: Applied StandardScaler fitted strictly to training data to prevent data leakage.

Algorithm: XGBoost Classifier equipped with optimal hyperparameters and scale_pos_weight to mathematically handle the 93/6 class imbalanc

The Trade-off (What this means)
Notice how the numbers shifted like a seesaw?

Setup 1 (Median) gave you higher Recall (48%). Because we filled 20% of the income data with a flat average, the model became a bit "dumber" and more aggressive, casting a wider net. It caught more real defaulters, but it also dragged in a lot more false alarms (Precision dropped to 37%).

Setup 2 (Model Imputer) gave you higher Precision (41%). Because the Random Forest gave XGBoost much more realistic, individualized income guesses, XGBoost became much more cautious and accurate. It made far fewer false accusations, but because it was playing it safe, it let a few more real defaulters slip through (Recall dropped to 42%).

Both are mathematically valid. Setup 2 is your most balanced "40/40" split, but if your boss told you, "I don't care about false alarms, just don't let defaulters slip through," you would actually prefer Setup 1!



STAGE 5.5: FINANCIAL SURVIVAL ANALYSIS & CALIBRATION
1. The "Raw Income" Logic (The Inverse Log Trick)
In the report, we must explain that raw_income is not a column from the original bank data; it is a Synthetic Recovery Variable.

The Reason: We previously applied a Log Transformation (np.log1p) to Income to squish outliers. While this helps the model learn, it makes it impossible to do normal math (like subtracting debt).

The Action: We used np.expm1 to "un-log" the income temporarily, turning it back into literal dollars ($) so we could calculate real-world financial stress.

2. Feature Engineering: Absolute Money Features
We moved from Relativity to Reality.

Absolute_Monthly_Debt: Instead of a percentage, the model now knows the literal dollar amount leaving the bank account every month.

Remaining_Living_Money: This is the "Safety Buffer." It tells the model exactly how much cash a person has left for food and rent after paying debts.

Why it helps: A 50% DebtRatio for someone making $2,000 is a disaster (only $1,000 left), but for someone making $20,000, it’s a luxury ($10,000 left). The model now sees this difference.

3. Probability Calibration (The "Honesty" Filter)
XGBoost is a "Ranking" engine, not a "Probability" engine. It’s great at saying "Person A is riskier than Person B," but it’s bad at saying "There is a 20% chance of defaul
t."

The Action: We applied Isotonic Regression Calibration.

The Result: We forced the model's "confidence" to align with "reality." This is why our optimal threshold moved from a weird 0.75 to a logical 0.21. It transformed the model from an aggressive guesser into a precise financial instrument.
